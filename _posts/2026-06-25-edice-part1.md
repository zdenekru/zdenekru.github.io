A few days ago I saw a simple pcb on my colleagues (L.Ch.) desk. A simple electronic toy, a dice with LEDs. It got me thinking "Can I do something like this? It shouldn't be that hard" and would be nice, interactive exercise. 

That was the start of project "e-Dice". The goals are: 
* 6d dice
* cyberpunk-ish estetic
* battery powered
* ATTinyXX as a core
* accelerometr as a source of RNG
* small footprint
* sleep enabled to save battery
* wake up on shake
* after some time wake up on button press
  

---

## 1. The Brains: Why the ATtiny1616?

When designing a modern battery-powered gadget, choosing the right MCU is critical. I bypassed the standard ESP32 or massive ARM chips for this project. They are absolute overkill and power hogs for an application like this. Instead, I went with the **ATtiny1616-M** (VQFN-20 package). 

Here is why this chip is a sleeper hit:
* **Memory & Pins:** 16 KB of Flash, 2 KB of SRAM, and up to 18 GPIOs – plenty for driving the matrix and talking to sensors.
* **Low Power Execution:** Active current down to microamps, and deep sleep modes that sleep soundly until a hard interrupt wakes them.
* **UPDI Protocol:** Single-pin programming and debugging. Look at the `UPDI Programming` block in the schematic – it only takes VCC, GND, and one single UPDI line to flash the firmware.

## 2. Sensing the Roll: LIS3DH Accelerometer via I2C

To make the dice responsive, it needs to know when it's being shaken or thrown. I integrated the **LIS3DH** ultra-low-power 3-axis linear accelerometer.

* **Communication:** Connected strictly via hardware **I2C (SDA/SCL lines)** with `4k7` pull-up resistors (`R1`, `R2`) pulled straight to the `VCC` rail.
* **The Magic Trick (Interrupts):** To avoid constantly polling the sensor (which wastes massive battery), the LIS3DH is configured to fire a hardware interrupt on the `INT1` line whenever it detects a threshold acceleration (a shake). This line is tied directly to `PA5` on the MCU to snap it out of deep sleep.

## 3. Power Architecture & Aggressive Power Saving

The entire device is pushed by a single **CR2032 3V coin cell** (`BT1`). Since a coin cell typically holds only around 210-240 mAh, the hardware architecture needs to be flawless:

* **Decoupling Bulk:** We have `10uF` (`C2`) and `220uF` (`C3`) capacitors right at the power entry. Coin cells have high internal resistance; when the LEDs flash, those capacitors act as a structural reservoir to prevent voltage dips that could reset the MCU.
* **Zero-Current Idle:** When the dice isn't moving, the MCU and accelerometer stay in sleep mode. Total quiescent draw is kept to a minimum, ensuring the battery lasts for months.

![e-Dice LEDs](/assets/img/edice/part1_schematicpower.png)

## 4. The LED Matrix (Multiplexing Layout)

To drive 7 independent multicolor dots for the dice faces, I avoided standard passive LEDs entirely. Instead of wasting GPIOs on row/column scanning or complex charlieplexing, I integrated the ultra-miniature **WS2812B-2020** addressable RGB LEDs.

Here is why this is a massive win for the e-Dice architecture:
* **One Pin to Rule Them All:** All 7 LEDs are **daisy-chained** together. The ATtiny1616 talks to the entire string using a single data pin, pushing a high-speed bitstream where each LED chops off its respective 24-bit color data and passes the rest downstream.
* **True Cyberpunk Aesthetics:** Since they are full RGB, we aren't locked into static dice colors. We can code dynamic shifting animations, color-coded state indicators, and crisp flashing patterns when the dice is "rolling."
* **The Power Caveat:** Every WS2812B has an internal constant-current driver that draws a tiny bit of quiescent current even when "off." Managing this standby power is going to be our main battle in the firmware to keep the CR2032 from draining.\
  
![e-Dice LEDs](/assets/img/edice/part1_leds.png)

## 5. The Hardware Safeguard: Minimalist Programming Interface

Look back at the schematic layout – you won't find any reset or boot buttons here. They are completely unnecessary for this architecture. 

Programming and debugging are handled exclusively via the **UPDI programming block** connected to the `Conn_01x03_Socket` (`J1`). Thanks to Microchip's modern single-pin Unified Program and Debug Interface, we only need to break out three pins: `VCC`, `GND`, and the bidirectional `UPDI` data line. This keeps the board incredibly clean and allows us to flash new firmware directly from PlatformIO without any bulky programming headers.

## Conclusion & Next Steps: Onto the PCB Layout!

With the schematic fully validated and every functional block locked down, the electrical foundation of project **e-Dice** is complete. We have:
1. A highly efficient 8-bit brain managing the control loop.
2. An accelerometer acting both as a hardware wake-up interrupt and a true physics-based source of entropy for our random number generator (RNG).
3. A single-wire addressable RGB LED chain that gives us maximum visual freedom with minimal pin overhead.

The real challenge, however, begins now. Fitting a VQFN-20 chip, a MEMS sensor, seven WS2812B-2020 components, and a massive CR2032 battery holder onto a tiny, hand-held, cyberpunk-themed multi-layer board is a masterclass in tight routing.
